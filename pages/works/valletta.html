The Symphony of Valletta
Audio Documentary
2019

The Symphony of Valletta is an audio documentary presented in four distinct movements, made as part of Culture Lab at Amsterdam University College where we visited Valletta, the European Capital of Culture for 2018.

Inspired by the genre of the city symphony which emerged in the 1920s as an experimental style documentary; an attempt to capture the ineffable spirit and characteristics of specific cities. As my first foreray into listening as a research practise I wanted to use the city symphony film as my guide for my movement throughout the city, to recognise Valletta through its urban soundscape. This approach was necessary to critically engage with listening and sound recording, this was one of my first engagements with this type of practise, and that there exists friction between capturing the soundscape of a city and what sounds I gravitate towards as an active listener. By researching concepts of soundscapes through M Schafer and E Thompson, I wanted to actively move away from selective and elitest conceptions of 'good' and 'bad' noise. The Symphony of Valletta is very much informed by my movement, as an individual exploring notions of ethnography through sound recordings and listening.

The piece is one continious whole, presented in four movements: ocean, city, caves, finale. These movements combine soundscape recordings, soundwalks, and interviews. Inspired by the idea of the symphony, I created a narrative where i could to engage with presenting recorded sounds in the form of a composition. 'Ocean' situates the listener geographically on the edge of the city, where after a while a few minutes walk brings you from ocean calmness into the center. 'City' brings us into the historical center, riddled with the sounds of tourists, information, construction, music where moments of sonic respite can be found within this chaos. 'Caves' explores the massive underground network present beneath Valletta, whilst it is not fully publically accesible, certain underground spaces have been opened up, for tours or artwork. Much of this piece is centered around recordings from a Susan Philipsz sonic artwork, which explores the caves as being used for bomb shelters during the Second World War. To create a closer to this piece, 'finale' uses all the sounds from the previous movements in combination to create a wall of sound in which the crescendo of the piece is reached. To recognise that all these sounds in combination are Valletta.

All sounds were recorded using a Zoom H2n over the span of two weeks, and edited together using Ableton Live.
